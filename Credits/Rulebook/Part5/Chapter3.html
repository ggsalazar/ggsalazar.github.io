Chapter 3: Armor

Protection You Wear

Armor is an adventurer’s best friend. It grants them added protection against the dangers they face on their travels, from the foes and monsters they fight to the various traps that they encounter. Without it, no group of novices could slay a roving band of Goblins, no soldier could survive an onslaught of Scorched, no outpost would have any hope of surviving an ork assault. Simply put, anyone who goes into combat without something to keep the swords, claws, and arrows away has little-to-no chance of survival.

Armor

There are various types of armor in I&A, and each comes with their own benefits and drawbacks, as shown in the table below. Certain class features will not be usable in certain armor types, e.g. Aetherians cannot cast spells while wearing Medium or Heavy armor/shields. The weight of the armor assumes the wearer is a Medium creature. Light/Medium/Heavy armor takes 2/5/10 minutes to put on and take off.

The type of armor you’re wearing also affects your Dodge score: none, Light, or Medium armor makes your Dodge score your Dodge Penalty + your AGI score + your DEX score, while Heavy armor makes your Dodge score your Dodge Penalty + your AGI score.

Armor Materials

In regards to what armor can be made of, there are 3 types of material: hide, metal and scale. There are 2 types of hide (beast and monster), 2 types of metal (steel and mithril), and 2 types of scale (dragon and monster), for a grand total of 6 different possible armor materials. In parentheses next to each type of armor are the types of materials that can be used to make it, and each of the armor’s statistics, such as its Armor Bonus and Cost, give only the statistics for the cheapest, simplest, most basic form of each armor (usually beast hide and/or steel). Materials can be bought or harvested, and a creature’s statistics in the Bestiary will say what kind of material, if any, can be harvested from it. The differences and details of each type of material are given below, in the Armor Materials table.

For the Additional Cost, the cost applies to the extra weight of the armor if it weighs more than the base and is added to the regular cost of the base armor; for example, a mithril chain shirt would weigh 15 lbs. and cost 10 + 15 (3 times 5) Aeons. If the material used weighs less than the base, calculate the cost as though it weighed more than the base and use that for the price. The cost to buy the materials for crafting can be found here.

Traveling in Armor

Traveling, especially on foot, is an exhaustive activity. For this reason, traveling in heavier armor will exhaust characters far faster than traveling in lighter armor. While traveling on foot in Medium/Heavy armor, a character takes fatigue damage equal to 5%/10% of their maximum Health (rounded appropriately, up if half) for every hour that they travel while wearing it.

Shields

Shields are always good whenever one feels they need some added protection. Shields come in a very wide variety of shapes, forms, sizes, and the like; however, for the sake of simplicity, all shields have been narrowed down to three categories based on their size, and each size confers a different bonus and has a different cost, as shown in the table below. As with the Armors table, each shield given assumes its most simple, basic form, and can be crafted or bought with different materials. However, unlike armor, shields can be made of wood, which is usually weaker than any of the given materials. For that reason, assume each of the materials given above gives an additional +1 to its Armor Bonus when applied to a shield.

If you are wearing a Medium or larger shield, you cannot wield anything other than the shield in the arm wearing it and you have disadvantage on all STR and DEX checks involving that arm.

Shield Bashing

They say the best offense is a good defense, and there are those who would take such an axiom literally. If you wish to use your shield as a weapon, that is entirely doable, and the base damage for each shield is as follows: 1d4 for Small, 1d6 for Medium, and 1d8 for Large. You must have >3 STR to wield a Large shield as a weapon, and the damage for each shield scales with the Weapon Skill of the same name, e.g. a character with 3 proficiency in Small weapons would deal 3d4 + their STR damage with a Small shield.
